What is RFID?
o Definition
➢ RFID (Radio Frequency Identification) is a technology that uses radio waves to
transfer energy and data between a reader and a movable item to identify,
categorize, track, …
➢ The item can be an object, a person, an animal, etc.
o RFID is fast, reliable, and does not require physical sight or contact between
reader/scanner and the tag.
o RFID, dates back to the invention of RADAR, where, during the Second
World War, fighter pilots cleverly maneuvered their planes to be remotely
identified by friendly radar operators, who distinguished them from their foes
(Identify Friend or Foe).
9

===== PAGE 10 =====
What Makes an RFID System
o RFID Tag (Transponder):
➢ Microchip: Stores data (e.g., unique identifier).
➢ Antenna: Allows for communication with the reader.
➢ Passive vs. Active tags:
❖ Passive Tags: No battery, rely on energy from the reader.
❖ Active Tags: Contain a battery and can transmit signals over a longer range.
o RFID Reader (Interrogator):
➢ Roles: Transmit and receive signals between the reader and the tag.
➢ Reader Types: Fixed, handheld, and mobile readers.
o Antenna:
➢ Role: Allows the exchange of data between the tag and reader.
➢ Types: Directional, omni-directional, near-field, and far-field antennas.
o Software (Middleware)
➢ Role: Software used to process data between the RFID system and enterprise
applications.

How Does RFID Work?
o Basic Workflow:
1. Reader: The RFID reader sends a radio signal to the tag, which powers it and
causes the tag to respond with its stored data.
2. Tag: The tag, powered by the reader's signal (for passive tags), emits a unique ID
or data when it comes into range.
3. Communication: The data is sent back to the reader, which transmits it to the
system for processing.
o No Line of Sight Needed:
➢ Unlike barcodes, RFID does not require a direct line of sight between the reader
and the tag.
o Multiple Tags:
➢ Multiple RFID tags can be read simultaneously, making RFID more efficient for
tracking large quantities of items.

Bar Codes
o The barcode is a binary code comprising a field of bars and gaps arranged in a 
parallel configuration
o History:
▪ The first barcode was developed by Bernard Silver and Norman Joseph Woodland in 
the late 1940s and early 1950s. 
▪ The first commercial use of barcodes was by the RCA/ Kroger system installed in 
Cincinnati on the behest of the National Association of Food Chains (NAFC).
▪ It was not widely used until the Universal Product Code (UPC) was introduced in 
America as early as 1973 and adopted by the US Supermarket Ad Hoc Committee.
o The most popular barcode by some margin is the EAN code (European 
Article Number), which was designed specifically to fulfil the requirements of 
the grocery industry in 1976.
o Today, the UPC represents a subset of the EAN code, and is therefore 
compatible with it.

===== PAGE 25 =====
1D Barcode Recognition
25
1D barcode is a combination of lines and blanks in accordance with certain coding
rules to represent certain information.
1D barcode only express information in the horizontal direction, and in the vertical
direction does not express any information. It usually retains a certain height to
facilitate the alignment of the reader.
Limited storage capacity, need to combine with the database.
The barcode size is relatively large, the space utilization is low.
Fault tolerance is poor, cannot restore the information after the corruption.

===== PAGE 26 =====
2D Code Recognition 
26
2D code uses black and white graphics on the two-dimensional
plane to record data, these geometric patterns express specific
information through a certain regulation of distribution .
Positioning point technology: 2D code usually has three positioning points for the
reader to identify. Because of these positioning points, 2D code can be identified no
matter what direction to read.
Fault tolerance: the information can be correctly restored when it does not recognize
all of the 2D code, or if the 2D code is defaced.
Large storage capacity. Up to 32KB.
High information density. Can store text, sound, pictures and other information.
Powerful error correction capability. 2D code can still be identified in the case of
50% defacement.
Support for encryption. Multiple security features.

===== PAGE 33 =====
RFID in 70s
o In 1948, Harry Stockman first showed “Communication by means of
reflected power”. It promoted the idea that “…considerable research and
development work has to be done before the remaining basic problems in
reflected-power communication are solved, and before the field of useful
applications is explored.”
o In the 50s and 60s, the anti-theft detection and electronic surveillance systems
popped up that all used RF technology.
o RFID wasn’t officially patented until 1973, in a landmark claim by Mario W.
Cardullo, who created an active RFID tagging system that utilized rewritable
memory.
o That same year, a passive RFID system was patented by Charles Walton,
who could unlock doors without a key—still used today in many hotel rooms.
o Until 1979, researches related to RFID remained within different laboratory
research. For the first time, it had commercial usage in animal tracking in
the United States. This was followed by motor vehicle toll collection in
Norway in 1987, followed by RFID tracking of US rail cars in 1994.
33

===== PAGE 34 =====
Setting Standards
o In 1999, the Auto-ID center was founded by MIT scientists to standardize
the RFID-encoded information format.
o This group had previously created the Electronic Product Code (EPC) for
RFID tags.
o Absence of related technologies like database management and manufacturing
process for small sized chips restricted the proper development of this
technology in the initial phase.
o Auto-ID group collaborated with Alien Technology to develop a fluidic
flow process that utilized vibrations to assemble the chips → massive
parallel assembly + lowering manufacturing costs
o In 2003, EPCGlobal systems came to being and they started standardizing
RFID from all possible directions.
o This was the biggest boost up that this technology received, followed by the
mandates and demands of the giants like Wal-Mart, US Department of
Defense, Gillette, and so on.

===== PAGE 35 =====
History of RFID (Summary)
o Early Beginnings:
➢ 1940s: RFID technology has roots in radar systems developed during WWII.
➢ 1948: The concept of passive radio-frequency identification is introduced 
by Harry Stockman, who wrote about the potential for using radio waves to 
transmit information for identification purposes.
o Development Milestones:
➢ 1960s: The first RFID tag was created for use in toll collection.
➢ 1970s: Early commercial use of RFID tags for asset tracking and livestock 
identification.
➢ 1990s: Introduction of EPC (Electronic Product Code) and the formalization of 
RFID standards.
➢ 2000s: Widespread adoption of RFID in supply chain management, retail, and 
healthcare.
o Modern-Day RFID:
➢ The rapid development of low-cost, efficient RFID tags.
➢ Growth of smart labels (integrated RFID chips with barcodes or sensors).

===== PAGE 39 =====
Simple logical processing
RFID has a very limited number of internal logic gates, so only a
simple logical processing can be made.
But the RFID system can use the basic logic processing ability of
tags to achieve some effective protocols and algorithms to improve
the system operating efficiency and security performance.
Reflection signal strength is affected by the distance and 
other factors significantly
Since the RFID tag itself is a passive device, the feedback signal
must be modulated by backscatter. Therefore, the strength of the
RFID tag reflection signal is susceptible to the surrounding
environment, including distance, reader power, signal interference,
and tag deployment density.

RFID Challenges (Cont’D)
o Anti-collision mechanism
▪ Conflict between tags, between readers, between reader and tags
▪ Traditional wireless communication protocols (such as CSMA / CA) are
not available
▪ RFID environment: tag identification protocols must be simple and
efficient
▪ How to effectively avoid conflicts, improve transmission performance,
and reduce the scan delay?
o Efficient information storage, retrieval and mining
▪ How to efficiently store, retrieve and mine ID, keyword information, and
sensing data carried by RFID tags in a distributed environment？
44

===== PAGE 45 =====
RFID Challenges (Cont’D)
o Make full use of the attenuation laws of backscatter signal to assist in
positioning and mobile behavior sensing
▪ Accuracy Low precision: within a few meters; High precision: within a
few centimeters
▪ Real time
▪ Stable Mean error, stability (confidence interval of error）
o Security certification and privacy protection
▪ Forged tags: how to validate?
▪ Malicious reader: how to prevent illegal access and protect user privacy
effectively？
▪ Existing algorithms require more logical processing units, e.g., AES
requires 20000-30000 logic gates
▪ RFID tags have only 5000-10000 logic gates
▪ How to implement authentication and privacy protection protocols in a
resource-constrained RFID system in a lightweight way?

RFID Today

===== PAGE 58 =====
Other Future Trends in RFID
o RFID and Blockchain:
➢ RFID is being combined with blockchain technology to enhance security,
transparency, and traceability in supply chains, especially in industries like
pharmaceuticals and food safety.
o Wearable RFID Technology:
➢ The use of RFID technology in wearables (e.g., smart watches, health-monitoring
devices) is expanding. This will help in personalized healthcare and asset tracking in
real-time.
o Advanced Anti-Collision Algorithms:
➢ With the increasing adoption of RFID in complex environments (e.g., warehouses,
logistics), more sophisticated anti-collision algorithms will be developed to handle
large numbers of tags being read simultaneously with minimal interference.
o UHF RFID for Consumer Applications:
➢ Wider use of UHF RFID in consumer products for applications like smart retail,
inventory management, and self-checkout systems, allowing for better customer
experience and operational efficiency.
o RFID in Smart Packaging:
➢ The integration of RFID with smart packaging will allow brands to improve product
tracking, authentication, and customer engagement.

Practical Insights: RFID with Arduino
o What is Arduino?: Arduino is an open-source electronics platform based on 
easy-to-use hardware and software.
o RFID and Arduino: Arduino can be used to interface with RFID tags and 
readers, enabling practical applications such as access control, asset tracking, 
and inventory management.
o Basic Components:
1. Arduino Board: For processing and controlling the project.
2. RFID Reader: Such as the MFRC522 module, commonly used in Arduino 
projects.
3. RFID Tags: Passive or active RFID tags.
4. Jumper Wires: To connect the RFID reader to the Arduino board.

Challenges and Considerations in Using RFID 
with Arduino
o Distance Limitations: RFID tag reading distance can be limited, especially 
with passive tags.
o Interference: Materials like metal and liquids can interfere with the RFID 
signal.
o Power Consumption: Active RFID tags need a power source, whereas passive 
tags do not.
o Data Security: Proper security measures must be considered when dealing 
with sensitive data.
